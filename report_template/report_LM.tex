\section{Introduction}
This project compares two strategies for word-level language modeling on the
Penn TreeBank corpus, evaluated with perplexity (PPL): training a Transformer
\emph{from scratch} (Part~A) versus fine-tuning a \emph{pre-trained} model
with a hand-implemented adapter (Part~B). For Part~A, a GPT-2-style
decoder-only Transformer~\cite{vaswani2017} is improved incrementally
through a learning-rate and architecture search, dropout, and a weight-tying
ablation, lowering test PPL from 35.68 to 33.07. For Part~B, the pre-trained
GPT-2 is fine-tuned with LoRA, training only low-rank adapters on the
$Q,K,V$ projections while the 124M backbone stays frozen, reaching test PPL
19.15. Both parts satisfy PPL${<}250$, and Part~B improves on Part~A by 42\%
relative, training only 0.7\% of the parameters.

\section{Implementation details}
Sentences are tokenized with the GPT-2 BPE tokenizer and padded on the right
(padding ignored in the loss); PPL is the exponential of the per-token
cross-entropy. Every experiment follows a greedy, one-change-at-a-time
search, selected on \emph{dev} PPL.

\textbf{Part A.} The search order is learning rate, then architecture
(\texttt{d\_model}, heads, layers, FFN size, one at a time), then dropout,
then a weight-tying ablation
(\texttt{lm\_head} tied to the token embedding)~\cite{press2017}. Tying does
\emph{not} help here: the tied model needs 20 epochs to converge against 7
for the untied one, reaching a worse PPL despite ${\sim}19$M fewer parameters
(dev PPL 44.47 vs.\ 36.86). Sharing the embedding and output projection
forces a single matrix to serve two conflicting roles -- representing an
input token and scoring output tokens -- which makes convergence harder,
more than the smaller parameter count helps generalization. The corpus is
not large enough relative to model size for overfitting to be the main
concern here, so removing parameters offers little benefit. Among the other
changes, increasing depth (\texttt{num\_layers} 2$\to$6) gives the largest
single gain in the architecture step (dev PPL 41.56$\to$37.99).

\textbf{Part B.} LoRA~\cite{hu2022} is implemented manually: each attention
layer injects low-rank matrices $A,B$ into $Q,K,V$, computing
$\Delta Wx=(\alpha/r)BAx$. $A\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, $B$ is zero-initialized, so
training starts exactly from the pre-trained model; only the adapters train
(0.7\% of parameters at rank 16). The search order is learning rate (rank 4,
$\alpha{=}8$ fixed), then rank $r\in\{4,8,16\}$, then $\alpha$ at the best
rank. PPL decreases monotonically with rank, as a larger rank gives the
adapter more capacity to adjust the frozen backbone. At the best rank, a
default scaling ($\alpha{=}r{=}16$) gives the lowest dev PPL (21.01), only
marginally ahead of a more conservative $\alpha{=}8$ (scaling 0.5, dev PPL
21.03, test PPL 19.14 vs.\ 19.15 -- within noise of one another), while
doubling the scaling ($\alpha{=}32$) is clearly worse (dev PPL 21.20): since
GPT-2 is already pre-trained on a far larger corpus than PTB, too large an
update pushes the fine-tuned model further from the pre-trained one than is
useful, eroding more of its general linguistic knowledge than it gains in
PTB-specific fit.

\section{Results}
Hyper-parameters are always selected on dev PPL, and test PPL is reported
only for the selected configuration, to avoid test-set leakage. Table~\ref{tab:lma}
and Figure~\ref{fig:lma} trace Part~A: every step except weight tying
improves test PPL, with depth and FFN size contributing the largest gains.
Table~\ref{tab:lmb} reports Part~B: PPL decreases monotonically with rank,
and at the best rank a default scaling ($\alpha{=}r$) gives the lowest dev
PPL (21.01), the selected configuration, with test PPL 19.15.
Figure~\ref{fig:lmb} traces the Part~B search step by step, the direct
counterpart of Figure~\ref{fig:lma}. Comparing the two parts, Part~B improves
on the best from-scratch model by 42\% relative while training only 0.88M of
125M parameters: Figure~\ref{fig:params} makes this explicit, with the LoRA
models reaching far lower test PPL than any from-scratch model despite two
orders of magnitude fewer trainable parameters (though they leverage the
frozen 124M pre-trained backbone). This gap is attributable to that backbone
already encoding general English syntax and semantics from a far larger
corpus, with LoRA only specializing that knowledge to PTB, rather than to the
fine-tuning technique itself. The two regimes also differ in convergence
(Figure~\ref{fig:conv}): the from-scratch models reach their best dev PPL
within ${\sim}2$ epochs and then overfit, whereas the LoRA adapters, far
harder to overfit on a frozen backbone, keep improving up to epochs
${\sim}14$--$20$.
